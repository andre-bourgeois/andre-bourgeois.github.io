If I asked you to think about a major technology company, what's the first one that comes to mind?

It might depend on how you're reading this - in a Google Chrome browser, maybe you arrived here via your favourite social network, maybe you're on a laptop running Windows - but odds are the first company you think of is a major American software company.

For decades, the world's largest companies have been just that: software-focused. Physical technology companies have always been around - Apple has made sure of that - but software has been the true driving force of the tech scene for nearly two decades.

This makes sense. Software is easier and cheaper to build and test, allowing for rapid iteration cycles. Through the internet, it's basically free to get into the hands of consumers. This is a perfect storm for venture building. Hardware, on the other hand, is difficult. Iteration cycles are slower, manufacturing is capital intensive, and that combination has historically left far less room for breakout hardware companies, especially when forced to compete for capital against the endless stream of software startups that have poured out of Silicon Valley since the early 2000s.

There have been exceptions. Tesla proved you could build a hardware company with a software company's ambition. Nvidia has become one of the most valuable companies on Earth making the chips that power the AI revolution, and that AI revolution itself is perhaps the biggest driver of renewed excitement in hardware, as the intelligence we're building creates insatiable, urgent demand for better chips, data centres, and physical infrastructure to run on. And, although that part of the hardware story is getting the most attention these days, there’s much more to it. Beneath it, less loudly, a much broader hardware renaissance is underway and the data is starting to speak for itself.

## The Startup Signal

One of the clearest early indicators of frontier technology's direction is Y Combinator, Silicon Valley's legendary startup accelerator that has funded Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe, and hundreds of other companies that went on to define their industries. In the Winter 2026 batch, 1 in 8 companies was building something physical - robots, drones, wearables, space hardware, biotech. That's 20 hardware startups out of 199 companies, described by analysts covering the batch as the sharpest tilt toward deep-tech in YC's history.

Some of the things being built feel straight out of a sci-fi movie: moon hotels, cattle-mustering drones, strength wearables designed by ex-Tesla engineers, and space solar arrays that unfurl to the size of a football field in orbit.

This shift isn't happening by accident. Engineers and researchers have spent decades refining the processes and technologies that made hardware so difficult to scale in the past. Industrial robot costs have dropped from $46,000 in 2010 to under $11,000 today. 3D printing has commoditised prototyping. AI-enabled design tools have accelerated development cycles that previously took years. A new generation of technical founders is applying software-style velocity to physical problems, and now has the infrastructure and market conditions to support them.

## Robots are Everywhere

The most visible expression of the hardware renaissance is robotics.

One of my earliest movie memories is Will Smith in 2004's I, Robot and just a few decades later, I can hardly open LinkedIn without seeing announcements of major partnerships and successful funding rounds from robotics companies. Venture funding to the sector increased by over 70% in 2025, nearing $14 billion. Among the standout rounds: Figure AI raised $1 billion in their Series C, and Apptronik - which counts Google among its backers - raised $935 million in Series A funding. The robotics market, valued at $357 billion today, is projected to reach somewhere between $1.2 and $1.5 trillion by 2030, with over 4 million robots already operating in factories worldwide.

How quickly might we bridge the sophistication gap with this kind of momentum?

## Cars Can Drive Themselves

Then there are the self-driving cars, not as a concept, not as a near-future promise, but as something you can hail on your phone in a growing number of American cities right now.

Waymo has recorded more than 20 million paid rides and driven over 200 million autonomous miles on public roads. It's handling over 500,000 trips per week across 11 major cities, with no human driver behind the wheel. In 2025 alone, the company launched in Austin and Atlanta, expanded its Bay Area service area nearly fivefold, and opened fully autonomous service at San Francisco International Airport. Plans are in place to bring the service to London and Tokyo.

## The Next Economic Frontier

Space is perhaps the most dramatic example of physical tech moving from science fiction to commercial reality.

SpaceX, a company that has fundamentally changed our relationship with the upper atmosphere, is weeks away from launching what is expected to be the largest IPO in history, targeting a valuation of $1.75 trillion and looking to raise over $70 billion. SpaceX generated $18.67 billion in revenue in 2025, a 33% year-on-year increase, driven primarily by Starlink, which now has 10.3 million subscribers across 164 countries and generates $3.26 billion per quarter.

But SpaceX is just the most visible piece of a much larger emerging space economy. McKinsey projects the global space economy - including satellite servicing, debris removal, and in-space manufacturing - could reach $1.8 trillion by 2035. Companies like Astroscale and ClearSpace are building first-of-their-kind orbital cleanup missions, targeting the hundreds of thousands of pieces of debris cluttering low Earth orbit. Others are working on space-based solar power, space tugs, and in-orbit refuelling - an entire logistics stack for a future where operating in orbit isn’t just fiction.

## Consumer Hardware is Finally Being Disrupted

Not all of the hardware renaissance involves rockets and robots. Some of it is much closer to home, with a new generation of companies quietly disrupting stale consumer hardware markets.

Nothing is perhaps the purest emblem of this. Founded in 2020 by Carl Pei, the London-based company had a simple, almost provocative thesis: consumer hardware had become boring, and people would pay for well-designed devices worth caring about. In 2024 alone, Nothing recorded $500 million in revenue with 150% year-on-year growth, and in September 2025 raised $200 million at a $1.3 billion valuation backed by Tiger Global, Google Ventures, and Qualcomm, firmly establishing itself in a market dominated by fierce brand loyalty and some of the world's largest companies.

Oura and Whoop - making discreet, screenless wearables for tracking health and wellbeing - tell a similar story. Both crossed $1 billion in revenue in 2025, shaking up a category that had barely evolved since the Fitbit. Oura, once a $20 million Finnish startup beloved by biohackers, is now valued at $11 billion and preparing to IPO. Whoop raised $575 million in March 2026 at a $10.1 billion valuation. Smart rings now account for 75% of all fitness tracker revenue, and one forecast puts wearable ownership at 1 in 3 American adults by the end of 2026.

## Hardware has become cool again

For a long time, building physical things was seen as the hard, slow, capital-intensive path. It was the domain of incumbents and engineers, not founders and venture capitalists. The past few years have quietly dismantled that assumption. The tools for building hardware have improved dramatically, the cost curves have moved in founders' favour, and a generation that grew up with software is now pointing its ambitions at the physical world.

The software revolution needed hardware to run on. Now hardware is having its own revolution, and if the data is anything to go by, it's only just getting started.
