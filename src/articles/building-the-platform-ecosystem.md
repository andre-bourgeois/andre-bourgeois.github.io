Each year, significant capital flows into new technology deployments around the world, much of it into new buildings or retrofits of existing ones.

Each year, the media fills with stories about the newest digital buildings, the most ambitious smart city projects, and the scale of investment behind them.

Each year, many of those projects quietly fail to deliver what they promised.

For building owners and operators, these failures are not just headlines. If well-funded, high-profile projects are falling short, what does that mean for your investment? Where are these projects going wrong, and how do you avoid the same fate?

Fortunately, there are ways to improve the odds. Not by spending more, but by focusing on the right things and making better technology decisions before a single cable is pulled or a line of code is written.

As we’ve explored in previous articles [LINK], modern buildings are best understood as platform-ecosystem hybrids, or simply, platform ecosystems.

A platform is the foundational technology or infrastructure that facilitates interactions and supports other products, services, and applications built on top of it. An ecosystem is a network of interconnected entities, companies, products, and users, whose relationships generate value greater than the sum of their parts.

In isolation, buildings are platforms: controlled technical foundations with standardised systems and defined interfaces. In the wider context, embedded in networks of owners, operators, vendors, occupants, and technology whose relationships create emergent value, they are ecosystems.

This hybrid nature creates an inherent tension. A digital building must provide structure and intentional definition while remaining flexible enough to accommodate emergent behaviour. Neither quality can be an afterthought; both must be embedded as fundamental design requirements from the earliest stages of a building's conception, and expressed in ways that remain agnostic to proprietary technologies and specific vendors.

Holding structure and openness in the same building sounds contradictory, but it’s not. It’s the result of four crucial decisions.

## 1. Define the Job to Be Done

The first decision speaks to both the platform and the ecosystem angles, and it extends well beyond the built environment. It is a concept borrowed from other industries that has only recently begun making headway in construction, as buildings grow more dependent on technology to function.

In the past, buildings were simpler. Structures had to be stable. Electricity had to flow. Plumbing had to work. The success criteria were physical and largely self-evident. Today, the use cases are far more complex and nuanced, and that complexity demands clarity of intent before anything else.

In practice, this means developing a grounded understanding of what the project is actually trying to achieve:

- What KPIs are you measuring, and how will you know if you have succeeded?
- What business decisions does this technology need to support?
- Why are you investing in this capability at all?
- What job is the building being asked to do, and for whom?
- Who has a stake in the outcome, and what do they need?

Before designing the technology, it is essential to understand why you are designing it. Getting the right people in the room early and interrogating the reasoning behind the investment, is what lets a team make decisions and select solutions that are fit for purpose. Without this foundation, no amount of money can ensure you hit the mark.

## 2. Establish a Common Language with Open Data Standards

Viewing the building as a platform brings us to the next two design decisions. As platforms, buildings are responsible for setting and managing clearly defined rules for interaction and extensibility.

The first of these is adopting and enforcing shared, open data standards. Clear naming conventions and semantic models create a common language across systems, enabling simpler integrations, more reliable analytics, and far less translation work whenever a new system or application is added.

The most widely adopted conventions include:

- Building Device and Asset Naming Standards (BDNS)
- Project Haystack
- Brick Schema
- Google's Digital Buildings Ontology (DBO)

These standards create a shared data language, allowing different systems, teams, and vendors to operate coherently within the same environment, however that environment evolves.

## 3. Provide an Independent Data Layer

The second platform-level design decision is supporting centralised, technology-agnostic access to building data. This is the role of an Independent Data Layer (IDL).

An IDL is a neutral, client-owned layer that makes building data available through a consistent, vendor-independent interface. By separating data access from any specific technology or vendor, it makes building architectures extensible, adaptable, and future-proof.

The practical benefits are significant. Designs become more scalable across buildings and portfolios. Clients can integrate best-in-class solutions rather than being locked into a single stack. And ownership of the data layer means ownership of the data itself, giving clients sovereignty over their own information and the ability to set, govern, and enforce the rules by which others access it.

Together, shared data standards and an independent data layer transform a collection of disparate systems into a coherent, scalable platform, one that is built to last and built to grow.

## 4. Choose Partners Who Play Well with Others

Viewing the building as an ecosystem surfaces the fourth critical design decision, one that the previous three help make possible but cannot guarantee on their own.

Ecosystem members must prioritise interoperability.

In practice, this means open APIs, flexible technology architectures, and business processes that make integration into the wider system straightforward and frictionless. Interoperability should not be a luxury, and it is not something to evaluate only after the contracts are signed.

A building platform can be well-designed, well-governed, and data-rich, but if its ecosystem participants are closed, proprietary, or difficult to integrate, the value the system can create will always be constrained.

## Good Decisions Create Better Odds

There is no way to absolutely guarantee the success of a digital building deployment. But the evidence is clear that most failures are not technical, they are decisional. The wrong priorities, the wrong partners, or the wrong assumptions, made too early and baked in too deep.

The four design decisions outlined here will not eliminate risk entirely. But they create the conditions for a building that is coherent, extensible, and capable of delivering the value it promised, at deployment and across its lifespan.

To summarise:

1. evelop a thorough understanding of your project's purpose.
2. Adopt and enforce open data standards and communications protocols.
3. Provide centralised, technology-agnostic access to building data.
4. Choose ecosystem partners that prioritise interoperability.

These decisions are about creating the conditions in which a building can actually deliver, at deployment and for years after. Get them right and the building has both the structure to function reliably and the openness to adapt as needs change. Get them wrong and you are left maintaining an expensive collection of systems that never quite cohere into a building.

That is the real promise of the platform ecosystem. Not a smarter building for the sake of being smart, but a foundation deliberate enough to give structure and open enough to let value emerge. Make these decisions well, and early, and you give the building every chance to deliver what it promised.
