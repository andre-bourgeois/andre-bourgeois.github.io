Is adoption more important now than it was a few years ago? Is it more important now than it was decades ago? Yes, and no. You could argue that adoption is the most important aspect of technology, full stop. Why create something that won’t be used? Why create something that isn’t useful? At a point in time when most things can be done, the real question we should be asking is, “What should be done?”

With every wave of technological change, in every industry, comes a wave of people trying to throw that innovation at problems before they have really understood either. It happened in the dot-com boom, it happened with smartphones, it happened with virtual reality and blockchain. In all cases, adoption, not the underlying technology, decided the winner. The companies built from or supporting these technologies are some of the biggest in the world today. Many more burned through any funding or reputation they had and ceased operation. 

Technology and innovation move in cycles. We see years of growth, years of uncertainty, years of stagnation, years of silence. They say there are decades where nothing happens and there are weeks where decades happen. Things are always moving forward in research labs or dorm rooms, but most of these advancements aren’t yet commercially viable and they fail to make their way into the eyes of the masses. In general, we’re blind to this constant undercurrent until it starts moving our boat. Because of this, we only see the major movements, or catch the gradual shape of innovation over long spans of time, often in retrospect.

But, generally, generations of technology and innovation follow familiar patterns.

1. A technical or commercial breakthrough occurs that makes some new innovation viable as a business.
2. People rush to throw this advancement at every problem they’re facing, or at places without problems, just to see what kind of benefit it provides.
3. Finally, there’s a period of consolidation or correction, some might use the word “culling”, that ultimately decides who survives.

We’re currently in the middle of a cycle with Generative Artificial Intelligence (Gen AI) and Large Language Models (LLMs).

## The Wave in Numbers

The AI wave is arriving at a scale that we’ve never seen before, and it’s still accelerating. AI companies took in roughly $202 billion in venture capital in 2025, close to half of all global startup funding. In the first half of 2026 alone, global startup investment hit a record $510 billion, more than the whole of 2025, with over 70% of it going to AI companies.

Most of this hype will not survive contact with reality. It won’t deliver what these kinds of numbers promise. A widely cited July 2025 study from MIT’s Media Lab (its Project NANDA initiative) found that 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots produced no measurable returns, despite an estimated $30 to $40 billion in enterprise investment behind them. The study has drawn some methodological criticism since, but the core finding tracks with every cycle we’ve seen before: the technology alone was never going to be the differentiator; adoption is all that matters.

## Why Adoption

Adoption isn’t a single problem with a single solution. It looks different depending on who you are and the customer segment you serve. Chip companies serve AI companies, AI companies serve end-users; this is a simplified value chain, but hopefully you get the picture. 

Nvidia is a clear example of a company in the AI industry that’s solved for adoption in its market. Its dominance was never solely about building the fastest chip. CUDA, its software platform, marked its 20th anniversary in March 2026 with six million developers building on top of it, according to Nvidia’s own count. That ecosystem - not the silicon alone - is what makes switching away from Nvidia so costly for its customers. This is what makes their products adoptable; this is what makes them sticky. Nvidia sold hardware and made itself the path of least resistance for everyone building AI on top of it.

Further down the value chain, the picture is messier. Some companies in the AI industry have found use cases that stick, ones that fit into existing workflows. Many more are discovering, slowly and expensively, that capable tools don’t guarantee use; they don’t guarantee adoption.

This is the core argument behind why adoption is so important, and it’s represented in the numbers above. Most AI initiatives fail, and a lot of the money being spent in this area is wasted because of that. Is it because the AI tools that exist aren’t good? Is it because they aren’t useful? Or could it be that we don’t fully understand the technology, or the problems we’re trying to solve? In almost every case I’ve come across, it’s the last one. The tools work. What’s missing is an understanding of the user, their problems, and whether or not these tools are fit for purpose.

## Why Now

We’re a few years into this particular movement: the initial shock of ChatGPT’s release, the first breathless wave of investment, the first working products. Investment is still accelerating, but the complaints are getting louder too. That combination, rising capital and rising scepticism, is indicative of the latter half of phase 2 in the cycle we described earlier. People are still dumping money into Gen AI and throwing it at every problem they can find. Companies which already have failed AI initiatives in their backlog are now paying other companies to throw AI at the problems they couldn’t fix themselves.

A February 2026 survey of nearly 6,000 executives across the US, UK, Germany and Australia found that 69% of firms are actively using AI, yet nine in ten reported no measurable impact on productivity or employment over the past three years. Even the heaviest users among them were only spending around 90 minutes a week with the tools. Access to the technology is no longer the bottleneck. Somewhere between installing it and integrating it into people’s workflow, most of that value is disappearing.

## What Happens Next

The culling has already started at the edges. Roughly 80% of so-called “AI wrapper” startups, companies that built a thin interface on top of someone else’s model, are expected to disappear within 18 months as margins compress and the big labs absorb any useful features natively.

Layoffs citing AI are now a running list in the tech press, just barely beating surges in rehiring from companies where AI initiatives failed. Klarna cut roughly 1,000 customer service roles to an AI assistant and started rehiring humans within six months once satisfaction scores fell. Ford brought back or promoted 350 engineers it had let go. Across the board, 32% of US hiring managers who eliminated a role primarily because of AI have since rehired for the same or a similar position, and 55% of leaders who made AI-driven cuts now admit, in hindsight, that the decision was wrong. None of this means the technology failed. It means the market, inside big companies as much as in the startup world, is sorting out who actually built something useful - something adoptable.

That’s the case for why adoption matters, and why it matters right now. The harder question, the one I’ve deliberately left alone, is how you actually design for it: the decisions that encourage adoption, and the ones that discourage it.
